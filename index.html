Egyptology:

The Study of Egyptian Culture and History

Why Study Egypt

Ancient Egypt was the most advanced civilization of the ancient world. They built the pyramids and magnificent temples. Their understanding of medical science was much more progressive than that of their peers and they perfected the art of mummification. Moreover, Egyptian mythology is among the richest in the world and through studying it we can come to understand how the ancient Egyptians understood life, death and the world around them. To put things into perspective, another ancient culture, the Greeks also revered Egypt as the progenitors of civilization. Compared to some of the earliest Greek myths like The Iliad or The Odyssey which were composed around 900 B.C, the pyramids of Egypt were built thousands of years earlier. All these reasons form the basis as to why it is important to study ancient Egypt today.

Timeline of Egypt

Below you'll find a timeline of ancient Egypt:

5500 B.C

Farming begins in the Nile Valley

3150 B.C

Upper and Lower Egypt are united. Menes the first Pharaoh of Egypt reigns

2686 B.C

The period known as the Old Kingdom begins. Giza pyramids and Great Sphinx are constructed at this time

2181 B.C

First Intermediate Period. Marked a downturn in Egytian culture. Lower and Upper Egypt became divided

2134 B.C

Middle Kingdom period. Prosperity returns to Egypt. Upper and Lower Egypt reunited

1549 B.C

New Kingdom period. Unprecedated prosperity as this period sees many great Pharaohs who secure their borders and build diplomatic ties with neighbouring countries. Pharaohs during this period include Hatshepsut, Tutankhamun and Ramesses the Great

664 B.C

The Late Kingdom period begins. Egypt is invaded and conquered by the Persians and Alexander the Great during this period

30 B.C

Cleopatra is the last Pharaoh of Egypt. Egypt becomes a province of the Roman Empire.

Myths and Mummification

According to Egyptian mythology there existed life after death. This idea stemmed from their observations of the world around them which was cyclical in nature. For example, they noticed that the sun always rose in the east and set in the west. Also, Spring and the growth of nature was followed by Winter and death. Therefore, the belief was that life could be achieved after death depending on if the right conditions were met. As such, the body had to be perserved through mummification and placed in a properly adorned tomb in order for the deceased to have everything they would require in the afterlife.

The practice of mummification began as early as 2400 B.C and continued all the way up until Egypt became a province of the Roman Empire. During the Old Kingdom period it was believed that only the pharaohs could attain life after death. However, around 2000 B.C attitudes began to change and it was thought that anyone could achieve this provided that they were mummified and buried accordingly. The Book of the Dead, a collection of ancient funerary texts, were entombed with the deceased in order for them to navigate through the afterlife and unite with the god of the dead, Osiris.

A combination of chemicals were used to dry the corpse which was left for 35-40 days. The organs of the deceased were removed and placed in canopic jars which were buried with them. The body was the covered in resin and linen, as many as 35 layers. During the Middle Kingdom placing funerary masks over the deceased became common practice. The most famous of these being Tutankhamun's, see our gallery page to view video evidence of this. The Egyptians also mummified animals, including cats. Cats were highly valued in Egyptian households for their ability to hunt rats and mice who infested granaries. Thus, they were entombed with their owner to assist them in the afterlife.
